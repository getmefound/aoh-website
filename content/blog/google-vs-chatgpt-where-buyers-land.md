## "Where do people even find businesses now?"

This is the question I get from almost every owner I talk to. They used to know the answer. You ran a Yellow Pages ad, you had a website, you maybe paid for some Google ads. Customers found you.

Now they're not sure. Their kid uses ChatGPT for everything. Their spouse uses Maps. Their oldest customers still call from a flip phone. They don't know where to put their attention.

Here's the honest answer for local service businesses in 2026.

## Most of your customers are still on Google

Don't let anybody tell you otherwise. Roughly 7 out of 10 local-business searches still happen on Google — the search bar, Google Maps, or Google Business Profiles. That share is *slowly* shrinking, but it's still the biggest single place customers go to find someone like you.

If your Google Business Profile is incomplete, if your reviews are old, if your map listing has the wrong hours — most of your missed revenue is right there. That's the dollar fix. Start there before you worry about AI.

## But ChatGPT and Google's own AI are taking the high-intent share

Here's what's changed in the last 18 months: when somebody asks a *specific* question — "who does emergency plumbing in Cherry Hill on weekends?" — they're increasingly asking ChatGPT or seeing Google's AI Overview at the top of the results.

Those questions used to scroll past 10 blue links. Now the customer often gets back a paragraph that names two or three businesses, with a recommendation. If you're one of the named businesses, you got the call. If you're not, you don't even get the click.

The share of search that's "AI-answered" is small in raw numbers — somewhere around 15 to 25% of local queries depending on category — but it's overweighted on the *most valuable* searches. The customer asking ChatGPT "who's the best mover for a one-bedroom apartment near downtown" is way more likely to book than the customer browsing the third page of Google.

So if you're trying to figure out where to focus: Google for volume, AI for high-intent.

## What "showing up in AI" actually means

I want to be plain about this because there's a lot of jargon floating around — "AEO," "GEO," "answer engine optimization." Most of it is the same idea dressed up.

When somebody asks ChatGPT about local businesses in your category, the AI is pulling from a few places to build its answer:

- Your Google Business Profile (yes, the same one that powers Google Maps)
- Your website if it has clear information about what you do, where, and for whom
- Mentions of your business across the open web — directories, news mentions, blogs
- Reviews — especially ones that say specific things about what you do well

Notice what's *not* on that list: stuffing your website with the word "best plumber Boston" 47 times. That hasn't worked on Google in years. It doesn't work on AI either. The AI doesn't reward keyword spam. It rewards businesses that other sources also describe the same way you do.

## What this means for what you should actually do

Three priorities, in this order:

**1. Make your Google Business Profile beautiful.** Hours right, services listed, photos current, reviews flowing in steadily. This is the single biggest lever for both Google and AI. If you don't do anything else, do this.

**2. Make your website clear about who you serve and where.** Not flashy — clear. "We do residential roofing in Westchester County, NY. We've been doing it since 2007." That sentence does more for AI search than a thousand-word "about us" page.

**3. Keep reviews fresh.** AI is biased toward businesses that appear active. A profile with the most recent review from 2022 reads as dormant to both Google and ChatGPT. We covered the velocity piece in another post — same logic applies here.

You don't need to chase AI like it's a new platform. You need to keep the basic, boring things in order. AI is reading the same signals Google is. The businesses that win on AI in 2026 are the ones that quietly kept doing the unglamorous work.
