# AOH Brand Voice — v1.0

**Owner:** Mike Egidio
**Audience:** Studio agent, Press agent, Coach, all human + agent writers producing AOH copy
**Read first:** [[AOH Ground Truth]]

---

## 1. Who we sound like

A neighbor who happens to know this stuff. Not a vendor. Not a guru. Not a tech bro. The voice of someone who spent 15 years walking teachers through software they didn't want to learn, now doing the same for shop owners.

Every sentence passes one test: **Would a 52-year-old auto-shop owner finish reading it without rolling his eyes?**

---

## 2. Voice adjectives (each with a behavior rule)

1. **Plainspoken** — Use kitchen-table words. If a 7th-grader couldn't read it aloud, rewrite it. "We answer the phone" beats "we provide omnichannel response infrastructure."
2. **Specific** — Always name the thing. Not "more leads" — "12 more booked appointments a month." Not "save time" — "stop calling back missed numbers at 9pm."
3. **Calm** — One claim per sentence. No exclamation points (the work brags for us). No ALL CAPS for emphasis. Tone of a contractor giving a fair estimate, not a salesperson closing.
4. **Owner-aware** — Reference what the reader actually does at 7am: opening the shop, missed voicemails, a one-star Google review on Monday. Never reference funnels, KPIs, or stacks.
5. **Stakes-aware, not scolding** — Name the cost ("missed calls go to a competitor in 3 minutes") without finger-wagging ("you SHOULD have a system!"). State the bill, then offer the fix.
6. **Humble-confident** — "This works." "This doesn't." Never "potentially," "could," or "may help." If we're not sure, we don't ship the sentence.
7. **Done-for-you** — Every sentence implies *we run it, you don't learn it.* Never invite the reader into the dashboard. Never use the word "platform."

## 3. Banned phrases (never use)

1. "AI department" / "outsourced AI department"
2. "AI-powered," "AI-driven," "powered by AI" (we say what it does, not what runs it)
3. "Leverage" (and "leveraging," "leveraged")
4. "Solutions" (we sell *work*, not solutions)
5. "Cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "world-class," "next-gen"
6. "Seamless," "seamlessly," "frictionless"
7. "Unlock," "supercharge," "10x," "game-changer"
8. "Dive in," "deep dive," "delve"
9. "In today's fast-paced world" (or any "in today's [adjective] [noun]")
10. "Empower" (and "empowering")
11. "Robust," "scalable," "enterprise-grade"
12. "Synergy," "ecosystem," "stack," "funnel," "pipeline" (in customer-facing copy)
13. "Let's chat," "hop on a quick call," "circle back"
14. "Imagine if…" (the before/after slider does this work — words don't need to)
15. Any vendor name (Apollo, Twilio, GHL, ElevenLabs, Smartlead) — see `feedback_no_technical_terminology_customer_facing.md`

## 4. On-brand phrases (signature phrasing)

1. "We run it. You don't learn it."
2. "The phone gets answered."
3. "Reviews go up. Calls get returned."
4. "You keep doing the work. We handle the part that costs you customers."
5. "No dashboard for you to log into."
6. "Set up in a week. Running by month-end."
7. "If it doesn't work, you don't pay."
8. "Built for shops that don't have a marketing person."
9. "We answer in your customer's language." *(Voice Agent only)*
10. "Here's what it costs you right now: ___."
11. "Here's what changes: ___."
12. "We've been the person explaining tech to non-tech buyers for 15 years."

## 6. Quick checklist (paste into every draft)

- [ ] Zero banned phrases
- [ ] At least one specific number, name, or scenario
- [ ] No more than one exclamation point on the page (ideally zero)
- [ ] A 7th-grader can read it aloud
- [ ] Reader never asked to log in, learn, or "explore"
- [ ] Cost is named, fix is offered, no scolding

---

## 7. Why these choices — rationale

**Pressure-tested against five frameworks:**

- **Joanna Wiebe (Copyhackers VOC)** — copy should sound like the customer talking to themselves at 2am. Every on-brand phrase is a complete owner-thought ("the phone gets answered"), not a marketer-thought.
- **Donald Miller (StoryBrand)** — the customer is the hero, the brand is the guide. AOH's "we run it, you don't learn it" makes this structural — Mike isn't the wizard, he's the contractor.
- **April Dunford (Obviously Awesome)** — generic positioning ("AI-powered solutions") makes you invisible. AOH's positioning IS the done-for-you operator vs. the SaaS that makes you learn. Voice rules enforce that distinction every sentence.
- **CXL / Conversion Sciences** — specificity converts. Vague claims don't. Hence the "always name the thing" adjective and the 7-of-15 banned phrases that are vague-fluff offenders.
- **NN/g content patterns** — 7th-grade readability isn't dumbing down; it's respecting cognitive load on a non-tech reader skimming on mobile between jobs.

**Comparable agency voices — what we borrowed:**

- **NiceJob** (review/reputation, SMB-facing): warm, jargon-free — matched the warmth, dropped the cheerleader energy.
- **MailerLite & ConvertKit**: friendly + concrete, never enterprise-y — matched the concreteness.
- **Stripe**: ruthless plain-language discipline — borrowed the discipline, not the developer-aimed voice.
- **Anthropic**: calm, doesn't oversell, admits what it doesn't know — borrowed the calm and the humility, dropped the research-lab register.

**What we deliberately didn't borrow:** big-tech authoritative voice. AOH's reader doesn't trust "world-class" — he trusts "the guy down the road who showed up when he said he would."
